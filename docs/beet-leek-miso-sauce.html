Roasted Beet & Leek Miso Cream Sauce

A silky, deep-magenta sauce for ravioli — earthy roasted beet and sweet braised leeks pureed with white miso, labneh, and a finishing splash of balsamic. All-pantry. Serves 2. About 20 minutes active; 35–40 minutes total with beet roasting.

Ingredients

  • 1 medium beet (~180g), peeled and cut into ½-inch wedges
  • 1 large leek (~90g), white and light-green parts only, halved lengthwise and sliced
  • ½ red onion (~55g), thinly sliced
  • 1 tbsp ghee, divided
  • ½ tbsp white miso paste
  • 3 tbsp labneh
  • 1–2 tsp balsamic vinegar (to taste)
  • 1 large handful spinach (~30g) — optional, adds nutrition invisibly
  • 2 tbsp nutritional yeast — optional, adds B vitamins, umami depth
  • ¼ cup pasta water (reserve before draining ravioli)
  • Black pepper to taste

Method

  1. Roast the beet: Preheat oven to 400°F (200°C). Toss beet wedges with ½ tbsp ghee and a pinch of salt. Spread on a baking sheet. Roast 30–35 minutes, turning once halfway, until tender and slightly caramelized at the edges. No need to wrap in foil — open roasting concentrates the flavor.
  2. Braise the leeks: While the beet roasts, heat remaining ½ tbsp ghee in a skillet over medium heat. Add leeks and red onion. Cook, stirring occasionally, until very soft and lightly golden — about 10–12 minutes. Do not rush this; soft, sweet leeks are the backbone of the sauce.
  3. Wilt spinach: If using, add spinach to the leek pan in the last 2 minutes of cooking. Stir until just wilted.
  4. Blend: Transfer roasted beet, leeks (and spinach if using), miso paste, and labneh to a blender. Add 2–3 tbsp pasta water. Blend until smooth and silky, about 60 seconds. Add more pasta water to reach a pourable but coating consistency.
  5. Finish: Return blended sauce to the skillet over low heat. Add balsamic vinegar 1 tsp at a time, tasting as you go. Add nutritional yeast if using. Season with black pepper. Do not boil — gentle warming only to prevent the labneh from breaking.
  6. Serve: Toss with just-drained ravioli. The sauce will tighten slightly as it cools, so use generously — it should coat each piece well.
Miso balance: Start with ½ tbsp miso and taste before adding more. Beet is naturally sweet; miso is salty. The goal is savory depth without the sauce tasting salty. Balsamic handles the acid role here — skip lemon juice, which would compete.
Reserve pasta water before draining — it contains starch that helps the sauce cling and emulsify. Plain water does not work the same way.

Notes on Key Ingredients

Beet root

Well established for endurance; emerging for other effects. Beets are one of the richest dietary sources of inorganic nitrate. Oral bacteria convert nitrate to nitrite; the body then reduces nitrite to nitric oxide (NO), which relaxes smooth muscle in blood vessel walls — reducing vascular resistance and increasing oxygen delivery to working muscles. Meta-analyses consistently show a modest but real improvement in time-to-exhaustion and VO₂ at submaximal intensities. Relevant for cycling. Roasting concentrates flavor and slightly reduces nitrate content compared to raw; the effect is present but mildly attenuated vs. beet juice. Beets also provide betalains (the pigments responsible for the deep red-purple color), which are potent antioxidants not found in other common vegetables.

Leeks

Well established as a prebiotic fiber source. Leeks belong to the allium family alongside garlic and onion, sharing their prebiotic fructooligosaccharide (FOS) and inulin content. Inulin selectively feeds beneficial gut bacteria (Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus) that ferment it into short-chain fatty acids. Leeks are also one of the better dietary sources of kaempferol, a flavonoid associated with reduced inflammatory cytokine production in human observational studies. Cooking leeks until very soft is important — it breaks down the cell walls and releases the FOS into the dish.

White miso

Well established for umami; fermented food benefits apply. White (shiro) miso is the mildest, least salty miso variety — the short fermentation time produces a sweeter, more delicate flavor than red miso, making it suitable for cream-style sauces where red miso would overpower. Adding miso off the heat or at low temperature preserves its live cultures. In this sauce, miso provides the umami bridge between the sweet beet and the tangy labneh — without it, the sauce would taste flat.

Labneh

Creamy without being heavy. Labneh is strained yogurt — higher in protein and calcium than regular yogurt, with a tang that acts as an internal acid balance. It does not char at high heat and should not be boiled, which is why the sauce is finished over low heat only. At 1½ tbsp per serving it adds creaminess without dominating the beet flavor.
