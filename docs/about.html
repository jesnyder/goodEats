About This Site

Jessipes for Good Eats is a personal reference for vegetarian cooking with an emphasis on nutritional density and high protein. It is organized as a living collection — recipes, techniques, and ingredient notes built up over time and kept in one place.

Dietary Approach

Vegetarian, no seafood. No meat or seafood. Dairy and eggs are used, though cheese is kept light.

Iron is a priority. Historically low iron, which is compounded by a vegetarian diet (plant-based iron is non-heme, absorbed at 2–20% efficiency vs 15–35% for heme iron from meat) and by regular cycling (which increases iron demand for oxygen transport via hemoglobin and myoglobin). Iron content and absorption are noted throughout. Vitamin C pairing and iron inhibitors (tannins, calcium) are flagged where relevant.

Fueling a cycling commute. Daily cycling increases caloric expenditure, glycogen demand, and electrolyte needs. Recipes aim to support sustained energy and recovery — not just protein density in isolation. Carbohydrates (rice, noodles, sweet potato, beans) are treated as performance fuel, not something to minimize.

High protein, intentionally. Each recipe prioritizes protein density. Where cooking technique affects protein content (e.g., freezing tofu, pressing paneer, soaking chia), that is noted and explained.

Nutritional density over calorie restriction. The goal is to get the most micronutrient value per calorie — not to minimize calories.

Fermented foods, daily. Kefir, miso, kimchi, and pickles appear regularly. Gut microbiome health is treated as foundational, not incidental.

Practical pantry. Recipes are built around what is actually in the kitchen. The Ingredients page tracks stock status and nutritional role for every staple.

Nutritional Priorities

Given the combination of vegetarian diet, low iron history, and regular endurance cycling, three nutrients warrant particular attention:

  • Iron — non-heme iron from plant foods has lower bioavailability than heme iron. Absorption is enhanced by vitamin C consumed at the same meal and inhibited by tannins (tea, matcha), calcium (dairy), and phytates (whole grains, legumes). The morning smoothie contains both matcha and kefir — consuming iron-rich foods alongside it may reduce absorption. Timing matters: separate high-iron meals from tea and dairy when possible.
  • B12 — not found in plant foods in bioavailable form. Kefir, whey, and fortified nutritional yeast contribute, but supplementation is strongly advised if dairy and eggs are not eaten at every meal.
  • Vitamin D — required for calcium absorption and immune function; commonly deficient, especially in people who spend significant time outdoors in variable weather (sun exposure in cold months is insufficient for synthesis). UV-exposed maitake mushrooms and fortified soy milk help; supplementation is commonly recommended for those at higher latitudes.

Philosophy on Nutritional Claims

Nutrition science has a replication problem. Many popular claims are based on small, short-duration, or industry-funded studies. This site tries to distinguish between what is well established and what is emerging or uncertain:

  • Well established — consistent findings across multiple independent, well-designed human studies.
  • Emerging / promising — early results in animal models or small human trials; plausible mechanism; worth tracking.
  • Limited evidence — few studies, methodological concerns, or primarily industry-funded. Include cautiously; hold claims lightly.

Where something is uncertain, that is stated. Where a popular claim is misleading (e.g., spirulina as a B12 source), that is corrected. The unknowns are as useful as the knowns.

Kitchen Setup

Oven Roasting, baking, broiling. Primary method for beets, paneer, and tofu finishing.
Stovetop Pan-frying, sautéing, simmering. Used for quick-sear proteins and sauces.
Instant Pot Pressure cooking. Fastest method for beets, lentils, and beans with good nutrient retention.
Blender Daily morning smoothie. Cold-blending only — heat-sensitive ingredients (flaxseed oil) added after.

Favorite Ingredients

A short list of the ingredients that appear most often and why:

  • Lentils and beans — the most efficient plant protein sources by cost and ease. Lentils need no soaking; beans are batch-cooked in the Instant Pot.
  • Tofu (frozen + pressed) — freezing before pressing increases protein density and creates a spongy texture that absorbs marinades deeply. Non-negotiable prep step.
  • Kefir — highest probiotic diversity of any common food. Used in marinades and the morning smoothie.
  • Miso — fermented umami base that works across cuisines. White miso is mild enough for dairy-based marinades.
  • Beets — dietary nitrate source with meaningful evidence for blood pressure and endurance. Roasted for flavor; pressure-cooked for speed.
  • Za'atar — finishes almost everything. Thyme, sumac, sesame, and salt in one blend; best added after heat to preserve volatile aromatics.
On tomatoes and beans: These appear throughout the pantry and recipes not just because they taste good, but because they are two of the most consistently well-studied foods for long-term health outcomes. Legumes in particular have stronger epidemiological support than almost any other single food category.
