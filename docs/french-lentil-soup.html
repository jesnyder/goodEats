Miso Leek and French Lentil Soup

A deeply savory, high-iron weeknight soup. French (Puy) lentils simmer with leeks and celery until just tender - they hold their shape and give the soup substance without becoming mushy. Miso is stirred in off the heat at the end, adding umami depth without bitterness, and preserving the probiotic cultures. Finished with lemon and za'atar. Very low fat, high folate, highest iron of any recipe in this collection at ~10.6mg per serving (59% DV). Serves 2 as a main meal.

Leeks need proper cleaning. Grit and soil hide between the layers of leek leaves, deep inside the bulb. A quick rinse on the outside does nothing. Cut the leek lengthwise from the light-green section all the way up through the dark-green top. Fan the layers open under cold running water, rubbing between layers to dislodge soil. Pat dry, then slice into half-moons. This is not optional - a single grain of sand in a smooth soup is disproportionately unpleasant.

Ingredients

Soup base

  • 150g French (Puy) lentils, dry ✓ in stock
  • 2 medium leeks (white and light-green parts only, ~200g), cleaned and sliced into half-moons △ out of stock — substitute: 1 extra yellow onion + 2 extra stalks celery for a similar aromatic base, or hold and make when leeks are back
  • 1 medium yellow onion (~150g), diced ✓ in stock
  • 2 stalks celery (~80g), sliced ✓ in stock
  • 1 tbsp olive oil or coconut oil ✓ in stock
  • 900ml water (or light vegetable stock)

Spice and aromatics

  • 1 tsp cumin ✓ in stock
  • ½ tsp turmeric ✓ in stock
  • ½ tsp smoked paprika ✓ in stock
  • ½ tsp dried ginger ✓ in stock
  • ¼ tsp black pepper ✓ in stock
  • 1 tsp liquid aminos ✓ in stock
  • 3-4 cloves garlic - when back in stock, add with the spices △ out of stock

Finish

  • 2 tbsp white or yellow miso paste ✓ in stock
  • Juice of 1 lemon ✓ in stock
  • 2 tbsp nutritional yeast ✓ in stock
  • 2 large handfuls kale or spinach (~100g total), stems removed ✓ in stock
  • 1 tsp za'atar per bowl, sprinkled over top ✓ in stock
  • 1 tsp sesame seeds per bowl ✓ in stock

Method

Instant Pot method (~35 min total, mostly hands-off)

  1. Rinse lentils and prep vegetables as in stovetop Step 1 below.
  2. Set Instant Pot to Sauté (Normal). Heat oil, add onion and celery, cook 4-5 min until soft. Add leeks, cook 3-4 min more until wilted and sweet-smelling.
  3. Add spices (cumin, turmeric, paprika, ginger, black pepper) and liquid aminos. Stir 1 min until fragrant.
  4. Add rinsed lentils and 700ml water (less than stovetop — the sealed pot loses no water to evaporation). Stir to combine. Cancel Sauté.
  5. Seal the lid. Set to High Pressure for 8 minutes. French lentils are firmer than brown or red; 8 min keeps them intact. 10 min gives a softer result if you prefer that.
  6. Natural release for 10 minutes, then quick release any remaining pressure. Open lid away from you.
  7. Stir in kale or spinach. The residual heat wilts spinach immediately; kale takes 2-3 min — put the lid back on (no pressure, just resting) if you want faster wilting.
  8. Scoop 3-4 tbsp of broth into a small bowl, dissolve miso into it, stir back into the pot. Do not use the Sauté function after adding miso — boiling destroys the probiotic cultures.
  9. Add lemon juice and nutritional yeast. Taste for salt. Ladle into bowls with za'atar and sesame seeds.
Why less water in the Instant Pot. Stovetop soup simmers uncovered and loses significant water to steam — 900ml in, roughly 700-750ml out in the bowl. The sealed Instant Pot loses almost none. Use 700ml for a thick, stew-like consistency. If you want more broth, add up to 900ml and accept a soupier result.

Instant Pot layered method — separate lentils, blended base, warming mode

A technique developed from making this recipe: cook the lentils plain and build the spiced broth separately, then combine on warming mode. This gives you more control over spice level and produces a creamier texture without added fat.

  1. Cook lentils plain: Add rinsed lentils and 600ml water to the Instant Pot. No spices, no aromatics — just water. High Pressure 8 minutes, natural release 10 minutes. The lentils cook into a neutral, starchy base you can adjust freely.
  2. Build the spiced broth separately on the stovetop (or in a second pot): sauté onion, celery, and aromatics, add spices, add water or stock, simmer 10-15 min. This broth is where you control flavor intensity — taste and adjust before combining.
  3. Blend ⅔ of the cooked lentils using an immersion blender or by transferring to a blender. The blended portion thickens the soup and creates a creamy base; the remaining ⅓ provides texture and visible lentils. Blending changes texture only — protein, fiber, and iron content are identical to whole lentils.
  4. Combine everything in the Instant Pot on Warming mode. Add blended lentils, whole lentils, spiced broth, greens, and any additions (beet, hummus powder, pineapple juice, kefir). Warming mode holds ~60–80°C — warm enough to meld flavors and wilt greens, low enough to preserve miso probiotics, kefir cultures, bromelain from pineapple, and vitamin C from lemon.
  5. Dissolve miso in a ladleful of broth as usual and stir in. Add lemon juice, nutritional yeast, kefir. Taste and adjust.
Why cooking together vs. separately in the Instant Pot affects flavor and nutrition.

Cooking everything together under pressure: Spices and aromatics infuse deeply and permanently into the lentils during the pressure cycle — the flavors integrate fully but are harder to adjust afterward. Under high pressure (~120°C), volatile aromatics cook off quickly, which is why pressure-cooked soups can sometimes taste flat. Heat-sensitive compounds — vitamin C, bromelain from pineapple, probiotic cultures from miso and kefir — are destroyed at pressure temperatures and must be added after.

Cooking lentils separately in plain water: Produces a neutral, starchy base that can absorb any flavor profile you introduce at the end. The spiced broth is built and tasted independently, so you can correct heat or seasoning before combining — you can't un-spice a lentil cooked under pressure. Lentils cooked in plain water also release phytates (antinutrients that bind iron and zinc) into the cooking liquid; whether you keep or discard that liquid is a tradeoff between iron absorption and flavor.

Pressure cooking reduces phytates regardless: Pressure cooking lentils reduces phytate content by 30–50% compared to boiling, improving iron and zinc absorption. This happens whether lentils are cooked with spices or plain — the pressure and heat do the work.

Warming mode to combine: The Instant Pot's warming mode holds ~60–80°C (140–176°F). This is below the threshold that destroys miso probiotic cultures (above 70°C), denatures bromelain from pineapple (above ~60–70°C), and degrades vitamin C significantly. Combining on warming mode lets all components meld over 15–20 minutes without any further nutrient loss from high heat. This is the right mode for any soup that includes fermented or enzyme-active ingredients added at the end.

Blending ⅔ of the lentils: Creates a naturally thick, creamy body using the lentils' own starch — no cream, butter, or flour needed. The unblended ⅓ provides contrasting texture. Nutritionally, blending is neutral: it does not change protein, iron, fiber, or calorie content. The starch granules from blended lentils act as a thickener in the same way a roux would, but with substantially more protein and fiber.

Stovetop method (~45 min total)

Step 1 - Rinse and prep

  1. Rinse lentils under cold water. Sort briefly — remove any debris. No soaking needed. Set aside.
  2. Clean leeks (see tip above). Slice white and light-green parts into half-moons, roughly 1cm thick. Discard dark green tops or reserve for stock.
  3. Dice onion. Slice celery.

Step 2 - Build the base

  1. Heat oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add onion and celery. Cook 5-6 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onion is translucent and starting to soften.
  2. Add leeks. Cook another 5-6 minutes until the leeks are completely soft and beginning to collapse. They should smell sweet, not sharp. Do not rush this step - fully softened aromatics give the soup its base flavor.
  3. Add cumin, turmeric, paprika, dried ginger, and black pepper. Stir to coat. Cook 1 minute until fragrant.
  4. Add liquid aminos and stir to combine.

Step 3 - Simmer the lentils

  1. Add rinsed lentils and water. Stir well. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer.
  2. Cook 25-30 minutes uncovered until lentils are tender but still holding their shape. French lentils should have a slight firmness when bitten - not mushy. Skim any foam that appears in the first few minutes.
  3. If the soup gets too thick before the lentils are done, add water in small splashes.
  4. Do not add the lemon juice yet. Acid prevents lentils from softening properly - always add after the lentils are fully cooked.

Step 4 - Wilt the greens

  1. When lentils are tender, add kale or spinach to the pot. Stir until wilted, about 2-3 minutes for kale, 1 minute for spinach.

Step 5 - Finish off heat

  1. Remove the pot from heat or turn it to very low.
  2. Scoop 3-4 tablespoons of hot broth into a small bowl. Add miso paste and whisk or stir until dissolved into a smooth liquid. Pour this back into the pot and stir through. Do not boil the soup again after adding miso.
  3. Squeeze lemon juice over the pot. Taste for salt - the miso is salty, so add carefully.
  4. Stir in nutritional yeast.
  5. Ladle into bowls. Finish each bowl with za'atar and sesame seeds.
Why miso goes in off heat. Miso is a fermented product containing live probiotic cultures and aromatic volatile compounds that contribute to its complex flavor. Boiling destroys both - the probiotics die above 70C and the aromatics cook off. Dissolving miso in a small amount of hot broth first (rather than dropping paste directly into the pot) helps it incorporate evenly without clumping. The same principle applies to adding miso to salad dressings, marinades, or any application where the miso is meant to stay raw.
French lentils hold shape; red and brown do not. French (Puy) lentils have a firmer outer layer and hold their structure through cooking. Red lentils dissolve almost completely into a thick puree. Brown lentils split and partially dissolve. If you want a smooth, blended dal-style soup, use red lentils and skip the blender. If you want a soup with defined lentils in a clear broth (this recipe), use French lentils and monitor timing closely - they go from firm to soft to disintegrating in a narrow window.

Variations

  • Add garlic when back in stock - 3-4 cloves, minced, added with the spices. This soup is built for garlic and the flavor gap is noticeable without it.
  • Fresh ginger (when back in stock) - 1 tbsp grated in place of dried. Fresh ginger is more citrusy and bright; dried is warmer and more concentrated.
  • Spicy version - add chili crisp (in stock) or a pinch of cayenne with the spices.
  • Heartier meal - serve over a bed of white or wild rice to soak up the broth, or with crusty bread.
  • ACV finish - replace half the lemon juice with apple cider vinegar for a slightly different acid profile. ACV has less citrus brightness and more fermented complexity.
  • Add feta on top - crumbled feta on each bowl adds protein and a salty, creamy contrast. Note that calcium in dairy competes with iron absorption; eat the soup first, add feta as garnish rather than cooking it in.
  • Evolved version (tested): cook lentils plain and blend ⅔ for a creamy base; add roasted beet (sweetness, earthiness, dietary nitrates), hummus powder (thickens, adds nutty depth and protein), pineapple juice (brightness, bromelain, dampens spice), kefir stirred in at the end (tang, dairy fat reduces heat perception), extra nutritional yeast. Combine all on Instant Pot warming mode to preserve fermented and enzyme-active ingredients. Results in a sweeter, creamier, more complex soup with lower perceived spice level.

Nutritional highlights

Strengths: This is the highest-iron dish in the collection at ~10.2mg per serving (57% DV), driven by two strong non-heme iron sources in one pot: French lentils (~5.8mg) and leeks (~2.1mg). Critically, this soup also contains vitamin C from lemon juice and kale in the same dish - vitamin C significantly improves non-heme iron absorption by reducing ferric iron (Fe3+) to ferrous iron (Fe2+), which the intestine absorbs 2-3x more readily. No dairy in the recipe means no calcium inhibition of that absorption, making iron uptake conditions here better than in the paneer bowl. Folate is excellent (~45% DV from lentils alone) - a critical nutrient gap in vegetarian diets. Miso provides probiotic cultures, B vitamins, and umami without adding fat. At only ~11g total fat per serving (mostly monounsaturated from olive oil), this is the lightest main course in the collection by fat content. High fiber (~9-10g per serving) from lentils and vegetables supports gut health and sustained energy release.

Data note: Leek iron varies between USDA datasets. The SR Legacy entry linked here (FDC 169246) reports 2.1mg iron per 100g raw leeks; the Foundation entry (FDC 2727584) reports 0.761mg per 100g. This table uses the SR Legacy value, which may overstate leek iron. If the Foundation value is correct, total per-serving iron drops to ~9.1mg (51% DV) — still the highest in this collection, still a strong source. The nutritional yeast link (FDC 2421410) points to a Branded entry, the only USDA match available; Foundation/SR Legacy have no nutritional yeast entry.

Watch points: Miso is the primary sodium source - 2 tbsp for the full recipe contributes ~1260mg sodium, or ~630mg per serving. This is moderate and well within safe limits for most people, but worth knowing if monitoring sodium. Lentils contain phytates, which bind iron and reduce absorption somewhat - the lemon juice in this recipe partially counteracts this, but soaking the lentils for 2-4 hours before cooking (then discarding the water) reduces phytates further if maximum iron absorption is the goal. Protein at ~26g per serving is solid for a soup. The carbohydrate load (~70g) comes almost entirely from lentils - appropriate for fueling exercise but a substantial amount for an inactive meal.

Acid timing with lentils. Adding lemon juice, tomato, vinegar, or any acid before lentils are fully cooked will prevent them from softening properly. Acid stabilizes the pectin structure in legume cell walls, locking them in a firmer state. Add all acid (lemon, vinegar, tomatoes) only after the lentils have reached the texture you want. This rule applies to all dried beans and lentils.
