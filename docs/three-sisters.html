Three Sisters Bowl

A North American chaat-style bowl built around the Three Sisters - squash, beans, and peas - simmered in a spiced tomato and coconut milk broth. The Three Sisters is a planting tradition of Indigenous North American nations (Haudenosaunee, Anishinaabe, and others) in which squash, corn, and beans are grown together: beans fix nitrogen for the corn, corn provides a climbing structure for the beans, and squash spreads along the ground to suppress weeds and retain moisture. This version adapts the core idea with peas in place of corn, butternut squash roasted separately for texture, and layered warm spicing in the chaat style. Serves 2.

Roast the squash separately. Simmering squash in the stew makes it soft and smooth - good for a thick broth. Roasting it first, then adding at the end, gives you caramelized edges and two textures in the bowl. Both approaches are correct; this recipe does the latter.

  • Salt to taste ✓ in stock
  • Juice of 1 lemon, to finish ✓ in stock

To finish

  • 2 tbsp nutritional yeast ✓ in stock
  • 1 tsp za'atar, sprinkled over the top ✓ in stock

Method

Step 1 - Roast the squash

  1. Preheat oven to 400°F (200°C).
  2. Toss squash cubes with olive oil, a pinch of cumin, smoked paprika, and salt. Spread on a lined sheet pan in a single layer.
  3. Roast 25-30 minutes until the edges are caramelized and the interior is tender when pierced. Do not stir - let the cut sides stay in contact with the pan.
  4. Set aside. The squash goes into the pot in the last 5 minutes to warm through without losing its texture.

Step 2 - Build the broth

  1. Heat oil in a wide pan or Dutch oven over medium heat. Add diced red onion and cook 5-6 minutes until soft and starting to color at the edges.
  2. Add grated ginger. Cook 1 minute - it should smell fragrant but not burn.
  3. Add tomato paste and spread it across the bottom of the pan. Cook 2-3 minutes, stirring occasionally, until it darkens from bright red to a deeper brick color. This step concentrates the flavor and cooks out the raw paste taste.
  4. Add cumin, smoked paprika, turmeric, and black pepper. Stir to coat everything. Cook 30 seconds.
  5. Pour in coconut milk and water. Stir well to lift any stuck bits from the bottom. Bring to a gentle simmer.

Step 3 - Add beans and peas

  1. Add the drained beans. Simmer 10 minutes to let them absorb the broth flavor. Add a splash more water if the broth reduces too quickly - it should be thick but pourable.
  2. Add green peas in the last 3 minutes. Frozen peas need only 2-3 minutes; fresh peas 4-5. Do not overcook - peas should stay bright green and slightly firm.
  3. If adding spinach or kale, add it in the final 2 minutes and stir until wilted.

Step 4 - Finish and plate

  1. Add roasted squash to the pot and fold in gently. Simmer 2-3 minutes to warm through.
  2. Squeeze lemon juice over the pot. Taste for salt.
  3. Stir in nutritional yeast - it thickens the broth slightly and adds savory depth.
  4. Ladle into bowls. Sprinkle za'atar and toasted sesame seeds immediately before serving.
Tomato paste technique. Cooking tomato paste directly in the oil before adding liquid is called "blooming" - the high heat drives off moisture and caramelizes the sugars, converting raw tomato bitterness into deep umami. The color change from bright red to brick is your signal. This same technique works for any concentrated tomato product and for spice pastes generally.

Variations

  • Add corn for the traditional Three Sisters combination. Fresh corn cut from the cob or frozen corn added with the peas. It adds sweetness and another texture layer.
  • Chickpeas instead of black beans - when back in stock. Chickpeas hold their shape better in the broth and are a more traditional chaat base. Black beans give a richer, earthier color.
  • Pumpkin seeds on top (when back in stock) - toasted, salty, and crunchy. A traditional finish for squash dishes in Indigenous American cooking and a strong zinc and iron source.
  • Mango pickle chutney on the side - this is what makes it fully chaat-style. A small spoonful of mango chutney on the side or drizzled over the top adds the sweet-sour-fermented contrast that defines chaat as a category.
  • Thicker consistency - simmer uncovered for 5 extra minutes, or mash a few spoonfuls of beans against the side of the pot to thicken the broth naturally.
  • Over grains - serve over white rice, bulgur wheat, or wild rice for a fuller meal. The broth soaks into the grain well.

Nutritional highlights

Strengths: This is one of the highest-iron meals in the collection at ~6.9mg per serving (38% DV). Black beans alone contribute 3.3mg - nearly as much as the entire paneer bowl. The tomato paste and lemon juice both provide vitamin C in the same dish, actively improving non-heme iron absorption. No dairy in this recipe means no calcium inhibiting that uptake - iron absorption conditions here are better than most other meals. Butternut squash is an exceptional source of beta-carotene (~457% DV vitamin A per 100g cooked). Very high fiber (~15g per serving) from beans + peas + squash supports gut health and slows the glucose response from the carbohydrate load. Folate from black beans (36% DV per 150g) directly addresses the key gap in vegetarian diets. Nutritional yeast adds B12 and complete protein. Turmeric with black pepper activates curcumin absorption - the black pepper compound piperine increases curcumin bioavailability by approximately 2000%.

Watch points: Coconut milk is the main nutritional trade-off - 100ml provides ~13g saturated fat (largely lauric acid, which has a better metabolic profile than long-chain saturated fats but still raises LDL in some individuals). If eating this alongside the paneer bowl in the same week, monitor total saturated fat from both dishes. Protein at ~26g per serving is solid but lower than the paneer bowl - add chickpeas instead of black beans when back in stock (slightly higher protein), or add a side of nutritional yeast or lentils to boost. The carbohydrate load (~77g) is high - appropriate and fueling for an active day with cycling, but a large meal for an inactive evening. No significant omega-3 source in this dish; the morning smoothie covers this.

Curcumin and piperine. Turmeric's active compound (curcumin) is poorly absorbed on its own. Black pepper's piperine inhibits the enzyme that breaks curcumin down before it can be absorbed, increasing bioavailability by roughly 20x at a dose as small as 1/4 tsp black pepper. This recipe includes both. The fat from coconut milk and olive oil further improves absorption since curcumin is fat-soluble.
Iron without inhibitors. This is the cleanest iron meal in the collection. No dairy (no calcium inhibition), no tannins (no tea or matcha), tomato paste + lemon juice provide vitamin C in the pot itself. The beans are the primary iron source (~3.3mg per serving) and the conditions here give non-heme iron its best shot at absorption. Eat an hour before or after any tea.
The Three Sisters tradition. The Three Sisters planting system - corn, beans, and squash - was developed by Indigenous peoples of the eastern and southwestern United States and Canada over thousands of years. Corn provides height; beans climb the corn and fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil; squash shades the ground, retaining moisture and suppressing weeds. Together they form a nutritionally complete diet: the corn provides carbohydrate, beans provide protein and lysine (an amino acid low in corn), and squash provides vitamins. This recipe adapts the tradition by substituting green peas for corn - peas are a legume like beans and provide more protein and folate, while losing the starch base that corn contributes. Serve over rice or bulgur to restore that balance.
