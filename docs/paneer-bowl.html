Labneh & Miso Paneer Bowl

A warm, umami-forward dinner built around pan-fried paneer in a labneh and miso marinade, roasted sweet potato, Brussels sprouts, beets, and red onion, sautéed spinach and kale with soy milk, nutritional yeast, and hummus powder, and a cold Szechuan peppercorn celery on the side. The cold celery plays the same role the cucumber does in the Asian Cucumber Salad — temperature and textural contrast against the warm plate. Serves 2.

Marinade ahead. Paneer needs at least 2 hours in the Labneh & Miso marinade — up to 4 hours is better. The beets can be par-cooked in the Instant Pot in advance and stored in the fridge until ready to roast with the other vegetables.

  • ¼ tsp white pepper ✓ in stock

Method

Step 1 — Marinate the paneer (2–4 hours ahead)

  1. Whisk together labneh, kefir, miso, liquid aminos (if using), grated red onion, lemon juice, cumin, smoked paprika, white pepper, and 1 tsp of the za'atar.
  2. Add paneer pieces and turn to coat well. Cover and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, up to 4.
  3. Hold back the remaining ½ tsp za'atar — sprinkle it on immediately after cooking, not before.

Step 2 — Roast the vegetables

Oven at 400°F (200°C).

If roasting beets from raw:

  1. Wrap whole unpeeled beets individually in foil. Place in oven. These take 45–60 minutes and go in first.

If beets are already par-cooked (e.g. Instant Pot):

  1. Cut par-cooked, peeled beets into wedges. Toss in olive oil and add to the sheet pan for the last 15–20 minutes of roasting — they just need to pick up color and heat through.

Sheet pan (add 35–40 min before serving):

  1. Toss sweet potato wedges with olive oil, salt, and a pinch of cumin. Spread on a lined sheet pan.
  2. Halve Brussels sprouts and place cut-side down on the same pan. Cut side down is not optional — it's what creates the caramelized, slightly charred face. Toss with olive oil and salt.
  3. Add red onion wedges to the pan. Toss in a little oil.
  4. Roast 35–40 minutes until sweet potato is tender and Brussels sprouts are deeply golden on the cut face. Do not stir — let the cut sides stay in contact with the hot pan.
  5. Add beet wedges in the last 15–20 minutes.
  6. When everything comes out, squeeze lemon over the beets. Cuts earthiness and improves iron absorption from the spinach to follow.

Step 3 — Dress the celery (30 min before serving)

  1. Slice celery thin on the bias — diagonal cuts about 3mm thick give more surface area and a better texture than straight rounds.
  2. Toss with Szechuan peppercorn oil, liquid aminos, white pepper, and a squeeze of lemon.
  3. Refrigerate until plating. The celery should be cold when it hits the plate — temperature contrast is the point.

Step 4 — Pan-fry the paneer (10 min before serving)

  1. Pat dry thoroughly. Press each piece with paper towel until the surface feels tacky, not wet. The labneh and kefir hold moisture — if you skip this step the paneer steams instead of browning.
  2. Heat a pan over medium-high heat with a small amount of oil.
  3. Add paneer without crowding. Do not move it until it releases naturally from the pan — 2–3 minutes per side.
  4. Flip and cook the other side. The surface should be golden-brown. Labneh is a soft cheese and does not char at high heat — the gentle browning you see is correct; do not increase heat trying to get a crust.
  5. Remove from pan. Immediately sprinkle the reserved ½ tsp za'atar over the hot surface — it sticks to the tacky coating and adds a fresh herby note on top of the cooked marinade.

Step 5 — Sauté the greens (right after paneer)

  1. Without cleaning the pan, add the sliced red onion to the residual heat. Let it soften briefly — 1–2 minutes.
  2. Add kale first (it needs more time than spinach) with a splash of soy milk. Let it soften in the steam — 2–3 minutes, tossing occasionally.
  3. Add spinach and toss everything together. It wilts in 60–90 seconds.
  4. Squeeze lemon over the greens.
  5. Sprinkle nutritional yeast and hummus powder over the top. The moisture from the greens helps them cling. Remove from heat immediately.

Step 6 — Plate

  1. Warm roasted vegetables as the base: beet wedges, sweet potato, Brussels sprouts, red onion.
  2. Sautéed greens (spinach, kale, red onion with nutritional yeast and hummus powder) alongside the roasted vegetables.
  3. Paneer on top of or alongside the vegetables.
  4. Cold dressed celery on the side — keep it separate so it stays cold and crunchy against the warm plate.
  5. A drizzle of any remaining Szechuan peppercorn oil over the full plate to finish.

Why the Szechuan peppercorn celery works

The cold celery plays the same structural role the cucumber does in the Asian Cucumber Salad. Sanshool — the active compound in Szechuan peppercorns — produces a numbing, tingling sensation by activating touch receptors (KCNK3/5 potassium channels), distinct from chili heat. This temporarily dampens the perception of both sharpness and warmth, making each bite of the warm plate feel more vivid afterward. The cold temperature and crunch of raw celery reinforce that reset between bites.

Celery is ~95% water and stays crisp under the dressing because it lacks the high pectin content that softens cucumber rapidly. Bias-cut slices (diagonal) give more surface area for the dressing to coat and a more interesting texture than straight rounds.

Strong meal for a cycling day. At ~36g protein per serving, this exceeds the threshold for maximally stimulating muscle protein synthesis (~25–40g per meal). The carbohydrates from sweet potato (~21g per serving) provide sustained glycogen fuel. Beet dietary nitrates convert to nitric oxide in the body, which improves blood vessel dilation and oxygen delivery to muscles — effects are most pronounced within 2–3 hours of eating.
Iron timing. Spinach contributes non-heme iron (~1.3mg per 50g wilted). The lemon juice added to the spinach provides vitamin C, which meaningfully enhances non-heme iron absorption. This meal does not contain the main inhibitors present in the morning smoothie (no matcha tannins, no large calcium load at the same time as the iron source) — iron absorption here will be better than from the smoothie.
Brussels sprouts and glucosinolates. Roasting at high heat preserves glucosinolates better than boiling — they leach into cooking water when boiled, but stay in the vegetable when roasted. Glucosinolates are the same sulfur-containing compounds found in kale, broccoli, and cabbage; they are converted to isothiocyanates in the gut, which have well-established evidence for reducing cancer risk across multiple epidemiological studies.
